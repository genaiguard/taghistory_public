Insurance-grade theft evidence: what adjusters actually need

An insurance claim on stolen construction equipment lives or dies on the quality of the evidence. The standard checklist most adjusters work from has four elements: (1) proof of ownership, (2) proof the item was on the insured's site or under their control immediately before the theft, (3) a documented timeline of when the item went missing, and (4) any movement after the loss that could support recovery or fraud-detection. Photos and serial numbers handle the first one. The remaining three are exactly where most contractors come up short.

Asset-tracking history closes that gap when it's structured right. A tagged trailer or generator has a continuous timestamped location record showing it was on the insured jobsite at 6:42 PM Friday, was stationary overnight, then crossed the geofence boundary at 2:14 AM Saturday and continued moving on a route that ended at a stable address forty miles away. That timeline answers all three of the adjuster's open questions in one document — and the same document, handed to police, is materially more actionable than a serial-number filing alone.

  • Export the full timestamped location history for the affected asset as CSV or JSON for the claims package
  • Pull the geofence event log showing the exact entry/exit times against the insured site boundary
  • Provide the movement-after-loss trail to the responding officer — recovery rates climb when the police report includes a current address rather than a last-seen point
  • Cross-reference the after-loss location with public records (tow yards, auction lots) before the trail goes cold

This isn't legal or insurance advice — every carrier handles claims differently and large-dollar recoveries usually involve adjusters and counsel directly. But contractors who run tagged fleets consistently report that the claims process moves faster, and recovery rates are higher, when they can hand over an actual timeline instead of a one-line incident report.
