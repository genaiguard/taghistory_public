Rollout pattern for hospitals and clinics

Start with the equipment classes that create the most search time and replacement spend - typically wheelchairs, pumps, and transport chairs. Standardize naming by department and asset type, set geofences for campus exits and controlled storage areas, and expand site by site as the workflow proves out.

Hospital asset tracking system: TagLogger vs CenTrak / Stanley RTLS

RTLS vendors — CenTrak, Stanley Healthcare AeroScout, Sonitor, Cognosos — built their economics around a six-figure facility install. Reader infrastructure (IR, ultra-wideband, BLE mesh, or active RFID) goes in the ceiling, active tags go on every asset, and the platform runs multilateration to compute sub-meter positions. Public deployment ranges run $200K–$500K+ per facility before tags or service. That math earns out for a narrow set of clinical workflows — OR case management, trauma bay positioning, infection-control contact tracing — where sub-meter precision is the actual requirement.

It does not earn out for the bulk of mobile equipment. Wheelchairs, IV pumps, SCDs, portable monitors, transport carts, vital-signs equipment, biomed-out-for-PM items — the question those teams ask is 'which floor / which department is it on, and did it come back to central supply?' The answer needs wing- or department-level precision, not sub-meter.

The pragmatic pattern many hospitals land on is both — RTLS in the narrow clinical zones where sub-meter precision actually changes outcomes, AirTag-based tracking on the bulk fleet where RTLS would be financial overkill. The two layers don't conflict because they ride entirely different signals (active tag readers vs Apple Find My relay).
