How rental teams start

Begin with the asset categories that drive the most shrink and margin risk - typically generators, compactors, light towers, and trailers. Set geofences for yards and depots, assign alert ownership, and build return-monitoring workflows.

As the process proves out, expand to additional inventory classes and branches using the same naming and alert standards.

Why this improves time utilization

Ghost fleet math: how 7% missing equipment compounds annually

Every rental yard runs a ghost fleet — equipment that's on the books but isn't on rent and isn't in any branch's physical count. Industry data suggests 5–15% of a typical rental fleet sits in this state at any given moment. The pieces that drift longest are the highest-margin: small-to-mid generators, light towers, compactors, scissor lifts. The branch manager doesn't notice they're missing because the contract status is closed; the rental coordinator doesn't notice because the asset isn't on a current ticket; and the corporate utilization report sees the unit as 'available' even though no branch has eyes on it.

The compounding cost is straightforward but rarely calculated honestly. A 200-unit branch with 7% ghost fleet has 14 missing units. At a $300/day average rate and 50% time utilization, the lost revenue per missing unit is $54,750/year — call it $50K to keep the math conservative. Across 14 units, that's $700,000 in annual revenue the branch can't book because it can't locate the inventory. This is before any actual replacement spend on units that ultimately get written off.

Tracking doesn't recover ghost fleet on its own — recovering it still takes branch effort, contract reviews, and sometimes legal action. What tracking does is convert 'missing' into 'located but not rented' or 'located but at the wrong customer site.' That pivot turns a write-off into a recovery action with a starting point. Most rental ops that deploy continuous tracking report ghost-fleet rates falling 30–50% within the first two quarters.
